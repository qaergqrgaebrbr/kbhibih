The Problem with Turf Grass

Traditional manicured lawns are beautiful but ecologically limited

🌾 Monoculture

Turf grass lawns usually rely on a single plant type, which limits the variety of habitat and food available to wildlife. Monocultures are vulnerable to disease and pests.

💧 High Maintenance

Mowing, watering, fertilizers, and pesticides keep lawns looking uniform, but they also reduce ecological value and consume significant resources year-round.

🏚️ Low Habitat Value

Dense turf offers little shelter, few flowers, and fewer resources for insects, birds, and other animals. Life needs diversity to thrive.

How Biodiversity Helps Wildlife

Rich biodiversity creates a feast for pollinators and wildlife

Native plants support more life because they coevolved with local insects, birds, and other animals over thousands of years. A richer mix of plants creates food, nesting sites, and seasonal blooms that keep ecosystems functioning.

💡 When yards include flowers, shrubs, grasses, and trees instead of only turf, they become vital stepping stones for pollinators and songbirds migrating through your region.

Better Landscape Ideas

A layered native garden with diverse plants and textures

🌿 Native Plant Beds

Replace part of the lawn with species naturally suited to your region's climate. Native plants are adapted to local conditions and require less maintenance.

🌼 Pollinator Strips

Add flowering plants that bloom across seasons—spring bulbs, summer wildflowers, and fall asters create continuous food sources.

🌳 Layered Habitat

Combine groundcovers, shrubs, and small trees to create a diverse landscape with multiple vertical layers where life can flourish.

What You Can Do Today

Getting started with native plants is rewarding and impactful
  • Reduce mowing in a small section of your yard and let low-growing native plants appear naturally.
  • Go chemical-free by swapping lawn chemicals for compost, mulch, and hand weeding where needed.
  • Plant native flowers that support bees and butterflies through spring, summer, and fall.
  • Create layers by keeping a smaller lawn for paths or play, and letting the rest grow with shrubs and trees.
  • Leave leaf piles in fall—they're hibernation homes for beneficial insects and small creatures.
  • Start small with just one garden bed or corner; you can expand over time.
